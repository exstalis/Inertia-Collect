### OPPRESION, DOMINATION AND THE MISERY OF THE POLIS

We often think of autocracy as a pre-existing condition. This applies not only to our social lives—where individuals exist in context—but also to political economy and how we understand it.

As a response to past autocracy, Keynesian welfare state policies were introduced as a remedy, yet they didn’t fully address the underlying issues (e.g., international limitations). The post-war solutions did not extend to a socio-political welfare model capable of overcoming economic depression. Instead, political economy shifted toward neoliberal politics, inaugurating an arms race in global markets. This led to intensified segregation within the political economy, further entrenching social disparities.

As Wendy Brown describes it:

“In ordinary parlance, neoliberalism refers to the repudiation of Keynesian welfare state economics and the ascendance of the Chicago School of political economy—von Hayek, Friedman, and others. In popular usage, neoliberalism is equated with a radically free market: maximized competition and free trade achieved through economic deregulation, elimination of tariffs, and a range of monetary and social policies favorable to business and indifferent toward poverty, social deracination, cultural decimation, long-term resource depletion, and environmental destruction.”

I find this description still relevant today. However, the term _liberal_ creates a conceptual limitation in understanding the broader impact of neoliberalism.

On the other hand, Iris Young’s notion of the “distributive paradigm” critiques the narrow focus of neoliberal thought. She argues that justice should not be reduced solely to economic distribution but must also consider oppression, participation, and systemic change.

Justice, being intangible and uncertain, mirrors human experience—shaped by context, negotiation, and action. Privileges based on race, gender, and class are central to understanding justice and political engagement. When economic restructuring promises solutions, it can also serve as a scapegoat mechanism, blaming individuals for systemic failures.

The issue arises when individuals disengage from diverse perspectives within political and economic frameworks. Excuses and ignorance toward oppression create a harmful belief system where suffering is justified as either an individual’s fault or a necessary sacrifice to maintain social hierarchies.

Ideas are powerful. They can change everything, yet they are difficult to change. Perhaps, instead of rigid adherence to ideology, we should encourage meditative reflection and second opinions.

Amartya Sen articulates this well:

	IN contrast with the utility-based or resource-based lines of thinking,           individual advantage is judged in the capability approach by a person's           ability to do things he or she has reason to value… The focus here is on the      freedom that a person actually has to do or be that which he or she may value.

This resonates deeply. Success within a group depends not only on resources but also on the emotional and contextual relationship an individual has with their environment. For instance, in a math class, if students associate numbers with personal experiences—such as a sibling’s age—they may find learning more engaging and intuitive. Similarly, social and economic inclusion requires meaningful connections beyond stereotypes.

If an individual from a financially disadvantaged background seeks to study, learn, or develop creative skills, they should be welcomed regardless of their appearance or status. True inclusion means fostering a sense of belonging and recognizing the value of diverse contributions.

In an increasingly heterogeneous world, acknowledging these connections makes us stronger. The capacity to say, “I am the only one for me, but I am among many,” allows us to expand our sense of community beyond ourselves.

### **Beyond Numbers: Rethinking Progress and Justice**  

We often measure a country's progress with a single number—like **Gross National Product (GNP)**—as if economic growth alone tells us whether people are thriving. But what if this approach is missing the point?  

Economist Amartya Sen argues that true well-being isn't about how much we have but about "what we can do"  with what we have. A person with wealth in a society without healthcare, education, or safety may be worse off than someone with fewer resources but greater opportunities. This idea challenges *the utilitarian tradition* which tries to fit everything valuable into one measurement of "utility," making us blind to deeper inequalities.  

This connects to Iris Marion Young’s critique of traditional justice, which often focuses on fair distribution—who gets what—without asking a more important question: Do people have the capability to live well?  

Sen and Young propose a shift from the **Distributive Paradigm**, which treats justice like a math problem, to the *Liberatory Paradigm*, which considers the real opportunities and freedoms people have to act, choose, and flourish. It’s not just about wealth—it's about whether people have the power to shape their lives.  

So, where does that leave us? It leaves us with a deeper understanding of justice—one that doesn’t stop at individual well-being but expands our sense of community. Recognizing that *I am the only one for me, but I am among many* allows us to see our interconnectedness. It reminds us that justice isn't just about lifting individuals; it’s about creating conditions where **we all** can thrive, together.

The liberatory paradigm is grounded in the real experiences of individuals rather than abstract, one-size-fits-all principles. Instead of pretending we can assess justice from a neutral, detached standpoint (like an Archimedean point or a “veil of ignorance”), it acknowledges that different social groups occupy unequal positions in society. Recognizing these systemic patterns of inequality isn’t just an added perspective—it’s a political necessity for achieving true justice.

Woodly’s critique moves beyond the welfare model of the past, where growth was the engine and social support a secondary fix. She advocates for well-being as a holistic goal: material sufficiency, yes, but also relational richness and subjective satisfaction. It’s not the possession of resources that defines us, but what we can achieve with them—a sentiment echoing Amartya Sen’s work.  This perspective invites us to consider how we might break from outdated paradigms and design economies that prioritize thriving in its many forms. Woodly’s analysis is both a challenge and an inspiration, urging us toward a more dynamic and inclusive vision.

The impact of material affluence is profound, but it’s not solely a matter of personal choice. Our political and economic systems are designed in a way that makes material security fragile for those who rely on wages rather than investment income. This vulnerability stems from several factors, the most significant being the growing monopsony power—or concentrated buying power—of firms as employers. Even in tight labor markets, this dynamic prevents workers from securing the wages and conditions necessary for thriving lives. This situation is worsened by a pervasive work culture in many parts of the world that demands constant availability and discourages complaints from employees—unless, of course, wages go unpaid.  

However, firms aren’t the sole or even primary culprits in this scenario. The widespread practices that enable monopsony have been permitted by federal governments, which have often turned a blind eye as working people are exploited by the collusion and disproportionate political influence of a small elite who live off wealth rather than labor (Ghilarducci 2019; Naidu, Posner, and Weyl 2018; Weil 2018).

The term **monopsony** refers to a market structure in which there is only one buyer (or a dominant buyer) for a particular good, service, or resource, while there are many sellers. This is the opposite of a monopoly, where there is one seller and many buyers. In a monopsony, the single buyer has significant market power to influence prices and terms, often to the disadvantage of the sellers.

This work culture has evolved within a neoliberal framework, where market logic is seen as the solution to all problems, and corporations are celebrated as ‘job creators’ whose interests are deemed inherently more important than those of workers. The belief in endless economic growth, tied to the fortunes of these firms, has taken precedence. Yet, empirical evidence reveals a stark disconnect: despite massive global economic growth, widespread prosperity has not followed. In fact, over the past two decades, there has been a sharp decline in people’s sense of well-being—a trend that decision-makers have, until recently, largely overlooked.  

Interestingly, research shows that active participation in community maintenance and governance significantly boosts well-being. As John F. Helliwell has noted, ‘People directly value their engagement with others, including their involvement in the process of making public decisions and delivering public services’ (Helliwell 2006, C43). For instance, psychologist Laura Wray-Lake and her colleagues have found that civic engagement enhances well-being among U.S. adolescents, especially when it involves face-to-face interactions, mutual support, and activities perceived as improving their local or broader environment (Wray-Lake et al. 2017). Similarly, Rashmi Choudhary and Deepali Sharma Gupta’s study of Indian adolescents revealed a strong correlation between civic engagement and subjective well-being, with a youth’s level of civic engagement accounting for 58 percent of the variance in their reported well-being


--- 

Bruno Frey and Alois Stutzer, both economists known for their work on happiness and political economy, found a significant link between democratic participation and well-being. Their research shows that in Swiss cantons where citizens have more frequent opportunities to engage in decision-making, adults report higher subjective well-being (Frey & Stutzer, 2000). Similarly, psychologists Michael Chandler and Christopher Lalonde, who study identity and mental health among Indigenous populations, discovered that indigenous communities in British Columbia with stronger self-governance structures experienced notably lower suicide rates. Their findings suggest that agency, cultural continuity, and community involvement play a crucial role in social well-being (Chandler & Lalonde, 1998).  
  
John Helliwell, an economist recognized for his research on social capital and well-being, further emphasizes that the social environment profoundly influences all aspects of life. Regardless of personality differences, people consistently value trust within their neighborhoods, workplaces, and public institutions (Helliwell, 2006). He argues that once the significance of trust and civic engagement is fully understood, it should guide policymaking in public service delivery. This means prioritizing the development of multi-use public spaces, fostering intergenerational connections in education and care, and creating structured opportunities for newcomers to integrate into communities—not just as recipients of services but as active participants in social and economic networks. This last point is especially critical: ensuring that newcomers have structured pathways to contribute to their communities enhances relational networks, reinforcing social cohesion and trust. Such policies don’t just improve individual well-being but also strengthen the collective foundation of society.  
  
Additionally, Helliwell critiques the traditional economic model that assumes individuals act solely out of self-interest. He challenges economists, who have long promoted the notion of “economic man,” to recognize the hidden costs of policies that overly depend on this assumption (Helliwell, 2006). This perspective aligns with Julie Rose’s argument that at a certain threshold, economic growth should no longer be the primary goal of society. Rather than continuously striving for material accumulation, economic strategies should shift toward sustaining meaningful engagement, relationships, and community-driven initiatives.  
  
This shift in perspective is crucial. Once basic needs are met, the focus should move away from acquiring more things and toward fostering sustainable economic structures. The true indicator of economic health should not be endless expansion but the ability to sustain and nurture local and micro-economies. By ensuring that economic policies support social bonds rather than isolate individuals in pursuit of wealth, societies can cultivate environments where people feel more connected, engaged, and ultimately, happier.  
  
At its core, the discussion challenges the idea that happiness is solely an individual pursuit. Instead, well-being emerges from relationships, shared experiences, and collective support systems. However, our current political and economic frameworks create barriers to these connections, often favoring large-scale economic growth over community well-being. Recognizing and addressing these structural limitations is essential for building a more holistic, sustainable, and fulfilling society.
