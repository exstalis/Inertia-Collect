A general method for establishing the consistency of abstract postulates was developed. The core idea is to find a model or interpretation where each postulate translates into a true statement about the model. For Euclidean geometry, ordinary space served as the model. This method was extended to find models for other systems, providing a framework to assess the consistency of their abstract postulates.

Consider the following set of postulates concerning two classes, **K** and **L**, whose specific nature is left undefined except as implicitly defined by the postulates:

- Any two members of **K** are contained in exactly one member of **L**.
- No member of **K** is contained in more than two members of **L**.
- The members of **K** are not all contained in a single member of **L**.
- Any two members of **L** contain exactly one member of **K**.

From this small set, several theorems can be derived using standard rules of inference. It can be shown that **K** contains exactly three members. However, the question remains: **Is the set consistent so that mutually contradictory theorems can never be derived from it?**

This question can be answered with the help of the following model:

> Let **K** be the class of points consisting of the vertices of a triangle, and **L** the class of lines made up of its sides; and let us understand the phrase 'A member of **K** is contained in a member of **L**' to mean that a point, which is a vertex, lies on a line, which is a side. Each of the five abstract postulates is then converted into a true statement. For instance, the first postulate asserts that any two points, which are vertices of the triangle, lie on exactly one line, which is a side. In this way, the set of postulates is proved to be consistent.

This geometric model demonstrates that the postulates are consistent. Similarly, the consistency of elliptic geometry can ostensibly be established by a model embodying its postulates. Here, the expression 'plane' in the axioms of elliptic geometry can be interpreted as the surface of a sphere, 'point' as a pair of antipodal points on the surface, and 'straight line' as a great circle on this surface. Each elliptic postulate is then converted into a theorem of Euclidean geometry. For example, on this interpretation, the elliptic parallel postulate reads: Through a point on the surface of a sphere, no great circle can be drawn parallel to a given great circle.

At first glance, this proof of the consistency of elliptic geometry may seem conclusive. However, a closer examination reveals that the problem has not been solved; it has merely been shifted to another domain. The proof attempts to settle the consistency of elliptic geometry by appealing to the consistency of Euclidean geometry. What emerges, then, is only this: Elliptic geometry is consistent if Euclidean geometry is consistent. This circularity is involved in demonstrating the consistency of a system that challenges the exclusive validity of Euclid. The inescapable question is: **Are the axioms of the Euclidean system itself consistent?**

Hilbert pursued another approach to address the issue of consistency in geometry. His strategy involved translating Euclid's axioms into an algebraic framework, using Cartesian geometry as a foundation. In this interpretation, geometric concepts were redefined in terms of algebraic relationships. For example, points in the plane were represented as pairs of real numbers, and lines or curves were described by algebraic equations. A straight line was defined as the linear relationship between two variables, while a circle was expressed through a quadratic equation.

Hilbert demonstrated that these algebraic representations satisfy the axioms of plane geometry. For instance, the geometric theorem stating that two distinct points uniquely determine a straight line could be verified algebraically by showing that a linear equation passes through exactly two points. Similarly, other geometric properties could be confirmed using algebraic models.
